What is the primary aim of Chan meditation? | The primary aim of Chan meditation is to achieve sudden enlightenment and a direct experience of one's true nature beyond all conceptual thought.

Who was Bodhidharma, and what is his significance in Chan Buddhism? | Bodhidharma is traditionally considered the founder of Chan Buddhism in China and is credited with emphasizing the direct transmission of insight beyond scriptures.

What does the phrase "Mind is Buddha" mean in Chan teachings? | "Mind is Buddha" means that enlightenment and Buddha-nature are not to be sought outside of one's own mind, which inherently possesses the qualities of a Buddha.

How does the practice of koan study work in Chan Buddhism? | Koan study involves meditating on paradoxical anecdotes or questions to transcend rational thinking and trigger a deeper insight into the nature of reality.

What is the significance of the "Flower Sermon" in Chan Buddhism? | The Flower Sermon represents the non-verbal transmission of truth from Buddha to Mahakasyapa, an event that emphasizes the Chan belief in direct experience over scripture.

How does Chan Buddhism view the concept of 'emptiness'? | Emptiness in Chan is understood not as nothingness but as the absence of intrinsic, separate existence, pointing to the interdependent nature of all things.

What role do 'Dharma talks' play in Chan practice? | Dharma talks are teachings given by a Chan master or monk that provide guidance on practice and insight into the nature of reality, often tailored to the students' level of understanding.

How is the practice of zazen central to Chan Buddhism? | Zazen, or seated meditation, is the heart of Chan practice, emphasizing the importance of meditation in reaching enlightenment.

What is meant by "sudden enlightenment" in Chan? | Sudden enlightenment refers to an immediate awakening to one's true nature, a fundamental aspect of Chan that differentiates it from practices that consider enlightenment as a gradual process.

How do Chan masters use unconventional methods in teaching? | Chan masters often use unexpected actions or statements to jolt students out of conceptual thinking and into a direct understanding of the teachings.

What is the role of silence in Chan practice? | Silence in Chan is valued as a means to quiet the mind, allowing practitioners to experience reality directly without the distraction of words and intellectualization.

How is 'suchness' understood in Chan philosophy? | 'Suchness' refers to the true nature of reality as it is, undistorted by conceptual labels or dualistic thinking, and is a key concept in Chan's approach to experiencing the world.

What does the term 'Dharma transmission' signify in Chan? | Dharma transmission is the process through which a Chan master recognizes a disciple's enlightenment and transmits the authority to teach and lead others.

How are art and poetry integrated into Chan practice? | Art and poetry in Chan serve as expressions of enlightenment and tools for teaching, often capturing the ineffable aspects of Chan insights in non-verbal ways.

What is the importance of the 'Four Statements of Chan'? | The Four Statements encapsulate the essence of Chan teaching: not relying on words and scriptures, pointing directly to the mind, seeing one's true nature, and achieving Buddhahood.

How do Chan practitioners use everyday activities as part of their practice? | Everyday activities are seen as opportunities for mindfulness and meditation, helping practitioners to maintain awareness and presence in all aspects of life.

What is the Chan perspective on the Buddha nature? | Chan asserts that all beings possess Buddha nature inherently and that enlightenment is the realization of this already existing truth.

How does the concept of 'no-mind' (wu-hsin) fit into Chan teaching? | 'No-mind' refers to a state of awareness without attachment to thoughts or sensations, embodying the freedom from dualistic distinctions between subject and object.

What distinguishes Rinzai and Soto schools within Zen, which originated from Chan? | Rinzai emphasizes koan practice and sudden enlightenment, while Soto focuses on zazen and the gradual cultivation of insight.

How is the practice of shikantaza performed in Soto Zen? | Shikantaza is a form of zazen that involves "just sitting" without any specific object of concentration, fully engaging in the act of sitting with no expectation of a particular outcome.

What is the significance of the Patriarch’s Hall in Chan tradition? | The Patriarch's Hall symbolizes the lineage and transmission of Chan from teacher to student, tracing back to the historical Buddha.

How does Chan Buddhism approach the concept of non-attachment? | Non-attachment in Chan is the practice of not clinging to thoughts, feelings, or sensory experiences, which leads to a state of freedom and equanimity.

What is the role of intuition in understanding Chan teachings? | Intuition is key in Chan, as it allows a direct, non-intellectual grasp of truths that cannot be fully expressed through language or rational thought.

How are humor and paradox used in Chan teaching? | Humor and paradox are often used by Chan masters to disrupt logical thinking, pointing students toward a direct realization of deeper truths.

In Chan, what does it mean to "kill the Buddha"? | "Kill the Buddha" is a metaphor used in Chan to express that one should not be attached to any image or concept of enlightenment, even to the Buddha himself.

What does 'spontaneity' signify in the life of a Chan practitioner? | Spontaneity in Chan signifies living in natural response to circumstances without being hindered by habitual patterns of thought or behavior.

How does Chan view the relationship between master and student? | The relationship is pivotal in Chan, as a master guides the student through direct transmission, often tailored to the student’s unique path to awakening.

What is the practice of 'hwa tou' in the context of Chan Buddhism? | 'Hwa tou' is the contemplation of the "word-head" or critical phrase of a koan, used as a focal point for intense meditation.

How does the concept of 'ordinary mind' relate to Chan enlightenment? | 'Ordinary mind' refers to the natural state of being without contrivance or effort, which is considered to be the enlightened mind in Chan.

What does the term 'finger pointing at the moon' signify in Chan? | It illustrates the idea that teachings and words are merely pointers to truth, and one should not confuse the pointer (finger) with the ultimate reality (moon).
