What is the definition of economics? | Economics is the social science that studies the production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services, focusing on how individuals, businesses, governments, and nations make choices about allocating resources.

What is the law of supply and demand? | The law of supply and demand states that the price of a good or service is determined by the availability of the good (supply) and the desire for it (demand), with the price adjusting to balance the two.

What is 'opportunity cost'? | Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative that is foregone when making a decision to pursue a certain action.

What is meant by 'market equilibrium'? | Market equilibrium is a condition where the quantity of a good supplied is equal to the quantity demanded, resulting in a stable market price.

What distinguishes macroeconomics from microeconomics? | Macroeconomics studies the behavior of the economy as a whole, focusing on aggregate measures like GDP and unemployment rates, while microeconomics analyzes individual and firm behavior and the mechanisms of supply and demand.

What is 'gross domestic product' (GDP)? | GDP is the total monetary value of all finished goods and services produced within a country's borders in a specific time period, serving as a comprehensive scorecard of a countryâ€™s economic health.

What are 'inferior goods' in economic terms? | Inferior goods are products whose demand decreases as the income of consumers increases, in contrast to normal goods where demand increases with higher incomes.

What role do 'externalities' play in economics? | Externalities are costs or benefits of economic activities that are not reflected in market prices and affect third parties who did not choose to incur that cost or benefit.

What is 'monetary policy'? | Monetary policy involves the management of a nation's money supply and interest rates by the central bank to influence economic activity, especially inflation, employment, and economic growth.

How is 'fiscal policy' used to influence the economy? | Fiscal policy refers to government spending and tax policies used to influence economic conditions, including demand for goods and services, employment, and inflation.

What is the function of the 'consumer price index' (CPI)? | The CPI measures changes in the price level of a market basket of consumer goods and services purchased by households, and is a common indicator of inflation.

What does 'comparative advantage' mean in international trade? | Comparative advantage is the ability of a country to produce a good or service at a lower opportunity cost than others, which can lead to beneficial trade and specialization.

What is the 'labor force participation rate'? | This rate measures the proportion of the working-age population that is actively engaged in the labor market, either employed or actively seeking employment.

What does 'marginal utility' refer to in microeconomics? | Marginal utility is the additional satisfaction or benefit received from consuming one more unit of a good or service.

How does 'inflation' affect an economy? | Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services is rising, eroding purchasing power and potentially influencing central bank monetary policy.

What is 'perfect competition' in a market structure? | Perfect competition describes a market structure where numerous small firms compete against each other with no single firm having significant market power, and where products are homogeneous.

What is 'price discrimination'? | Price discrimination occurs when a firm sells the same product at different prices to different groups of consumers, based on their willingness to pay, rather than differences in production costs.

What is 'game theory' and how is it applied in economics? | Game theory studies strategic interactions where the outcome depends on the actions of all participants, and it's used in economics to model competitive behaviors among firms and individuals.

What is the 'multiplier effect'? | The multiplier effect refers to the proportionate increase in final income that results from an injection of spending.

How does 'unemployment rate' impact the economy? | The unemployment rate is the percentage of the labor force that is jobless and actively seeking employment; high rates can indicate economic distress, while extremely low rates can signal an overheating economy.

What is the concept of 'velocity of money'? | The velocity of money is the rate at which money circulates through the economy and is used to purchase goods and services, reflecting the activity level of economic transactions.

What does 'liquidity' mean in finance? | Liquidity refers to how easily assets can be converted into cash without affecting their market price.

What is a 'progressive tax system'? | A progressive tax system is one where the tax rate increases as the taxable base amount increases, resulting in higher income earners paying a larger percentage of their income in taxes.

What is the difference between 'nominal' and 'real' economic measures? | Nominal measures are unadjusted for inflation, while real measures are adjusted to reflect changes in price levels, giving a more accurate picture of economic performance.

What are 'public goods' and why are they unique in economics? | Public goods are goods that are non-excludable and non-rivalrous, meaning they can be used by many people simultaneously without being depleted, like national defense or public parks.

How do 'tariffs' and 'quotas' affect international trade? | Tariffs are taxes imposed on imported goods, and quotas are limits on the quantity of goods that can be imported, both used to protect domestic industries but can lead to trade wars and reduced economic efficiency.
